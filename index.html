THE CHALLENGE

"For 44,000 Deaf Malaysians, education is a one-way street. While captions allow consumption, students remain voiceless without scarce interpreters, as existing tech cannot handle complex BIM sentences for real-time discourse."

Communication Barrier

Over 40,000 DHH Malaysians face social exclusion due to a lack of real-time BIM interpretation in essential daily sectors.

Challenge in Education

University students suffer from one-way communication, unable to ask questions or participate in discussions without human interpreters.

Failure of Human Solutions

With only 60 certified interpreters nationwide, services are scarce and prohibitively expensive for daily academic needs.

Failure of Existing Technology

Existing apps are merely static dictionaries or focus on ASL, failing to support continuous, complex BIM sentences.

Challenges Faced

Dataset Inconsistency

BIM Sign Bank videos lack standardized framing (varying between hand-only and half-body shots) and utilize a single camera angle, complicating spatial tracking.

Compute & Time Trade-offs

Training a model on the entire 2,966-word BIM lexicon was impossible given the hackathon's tight timeframe and available compute resources. We had to downsample to a curated subset of high-frequency academic terms.

Model Hallucinations

The current sequence-prediction model occasionally misclassifies ambiguous gestures, resulting in incorrect raw word outputs before passing through the GenAI semantic layer.

Qualitative Transformation

Academic Equality

Deaf students no longer have to be passive listeners. By giving them a "voice" in real-time, they can ask questions, participate in group assignments, and debate with professors, drastically improving their academic performance and confidence.

Financial Relief

Students and universities no longer need to hire expensive human interpreters (RM 100+/hour) for everyday study sessions or casual meetings, democratizing access to education regardless of financial status.

Career Readiness

The biggest barrier to Deaf employment is the interview process and office communication. A portable AI translator bridges this gap, allowing DHH graduates to enter the mainstream workforce without requiring special employer accommodations.
